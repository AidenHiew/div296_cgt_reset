Reset your SMSF's Div 296 cost base — or not? See the tax difference before you decide.

From the 2026-27 income year, Division 296 adds tax on super balances above $3 million. A fund can elect to reset its Div 296 cost base to market value at 30 June 2026. For some funds that saves tax — for funds holding assets in an unrealised loss, it can create tax that wasn't there. This model shows you both outcomes, side by side.

Division 296 in plain English

1

A new tax above $3m

If your TSB is over $3 million, Div 296 applies an extra 15% on the share of earnings attributable to the slice between $3m and $10m, and an extra 25% on the slice above $10m. It's on top of the normal 15% fund tax.

2

The one-time reset election

A fund can elect to step its Div 296 cost base up to market value at 30 June 2026, instead of original cost. The election is lodged with the 2026-27 return, but the value is fixed at 30 June 2026. It's irreversible and applies to the whole balance. Ordinary CGT is unaffected — it always uses original cost.

3

The "reset trap"

For an asset sitting in an unrealised loss, resetting the cost base down to market value turns a future loss into a future Div 296 gain — creating tax that didn't exist. The trap is the whole reason to model this before electing.

For the accountant — the mechanics in full
  • Two-band tax. A member's tax = earnings × split × (band1 × 15% + band2 × 25%), where band1 is the proportion of TSB in the $3m–$10m slice and band2 the proportion above $10m. The $10m / +25% tier is enacted law and always applies.
  • Intra-year netting (s102-5). Capital gains and losses net within the income year at the fund level, for both ordinary CGT and Div 296. Div 296 fund earnings = MAX(0, Σ adjusted gains) — floored at zero.
  • 1/3 CGT discount. Applies to assets held > 12 months, to gains only (never losses), on both the ordinary and Div 296 sides.
  • Per-asset allocation. Each asset's Div 296 tax is its share of the headline, pro-rata by positive adjusted gain. Loss assets bear $0 — even when they reduce the headline at fund level.
  • Not modelled: prior-year carry-forward losses, pension phase (assumes 100% accumulation), and unrealised TSB movement under the no-reset path (compares realised vs realised). See the workbook's Notes tab.

⚠

Why this needs modelling first

The election is irreversible and applies to your whole balance. It's made with your 2026-27 return, but the cost base is locked to market value at 30 June 2026 — so the valuation date is what you're racing. Electing the reset to cut tax on your gains can simultaneously create Div 296 tax on assets currently in a loss. The only way to know the net effect for your fund is to model both paths on real holdings. Selling purely for the tax outcome also sits in anti-avoidance territory (Part IVA / TR 2008/1); always confirm with your tax agent.
